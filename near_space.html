How do we reach near space?


Near space is the name given to the region above the altitudes for commercial flights and below the orbit of the satellites. This region is roughly from the altitude of 20km to 100km. Near space gives an opportunity of studying several phenomena which will require great resource and time to replicate on the ground. It also gives the chance to study outer space without truly leaving the earth while avoiding the obstacles created due to thick layer of earth between ground and near space.

In the near space region, the air is too thin to support the commercial flights and still dense enough to cause aerodynamic drag in the satellites, which will lead to a loss of energy of the orbit of satellites. To overcome such problems, high altitude balloons (HABs) are used to carry out research in the near-space region. The experimental payload is attached to a HAB, which is filled with either hydrogen or helium. Once airborne the path of the balloon cannot be controlled, as it is dependent on the wind pattern. However, the altitude of the balloon can be controlled to a certain extent by maintaining necessary pressure for the weight of the payload. For our experiment, the payload will be sent to near-space by the National Balloon Facility at TIFR-Hyderabad who will also provide the required balloon. The experimental setup will collect data from ground level until a height of around 22km where it will float for around three hours. The expected date for launch is between 15th December to 25th December.
